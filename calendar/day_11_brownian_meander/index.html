Definition#

Let \(W=\{W(t), t\geq 0\}\) be a standard one-dimensionalÂ Brownian motion, and

\[\tau = \sup\{ t\in \[0,1\] : W(t) = 0 \},\]

i.e. the last time beforeÂ \(t=1\) when W visits zero. Then the Brownian meander is defined by the following expression

\[X(t) = \frac{1}{1 - \tau} | W(\tau + t(1-\tau)) |, \qquad t \in \[0,1\].\]

That is, given the last time before 1 that a standard Brownian motion visitsÂ zero (note that \(\tau <1\) almost surely), we snip off and discard the trajectory beforeÂ \(\tau\), and scale the remaining part so that it spans a time interval of length 1. The scaling factor for the spatial axis must be square root of the scaling factor for the time axis. The process resulting from this snip-and-scale procedure is a Brownian meander.
